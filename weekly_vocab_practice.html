Exercise 1A — Situational reading

What's really happening here?

Read each mini-situation. Identify which vocabulary word or phrasal verb best describes what's happening — say it out loud and explain your reasoning in one sentence.

AYour colleague João was supposed to send you the report on Tuesday. It's now Friday and he still hasn't sent it. Every time you bring it up, he says "I'll do it tomorrow."
BIn every team meeting, whenever Ana speaks, the other two colleagues start side conversations and look at their phones. Ana has noticed she's never included in the group chat either.
CRicardo walks into every client call with a calm, open posture. He remembers people's names, asks follow-up questions, and clients always leave feeling understood.
DThe whole team is overwhelmed. Three deadlines are due on the same day. Everyone is stressed — not just you.
ECarla has been working 14-hour days for three months straight. She's started making mistakes, sleeping badly, and losing motivation for everything.
FAfter the project meeting, Marcos sent a quick message to each team member to check how they were doing with their tasks and if they needed anything.

Example answer

Situation A → "João is definitely putting off the report. He keeps postponing it and giving off a vibe that he doesn't want to deal with the problem."

💡

Challenge: after identifying the word, add one more sentence using a second vocabulary item. E.g. "…and this really gets under my skin."

Exercise 2A — Paraphrase challenge

Say it differently

Rewrite each sentence using the phrasal verb or idiom in brackets. Say your new sentence out loud — the meaning must stay the same.

1"I've been avoiding sending that invoice for two weeks." → use put off
2"My new student seems cold but actually she's just shy." → use give off
3"After the class I always send a quick message to see how they're doing." → use check in with
4"When my manager left, I had to start leading the team." → use step up
5"All freelancers deal with the same problem: irregular income." → use in the same boat

Example

Original: "I've been avoiding sending that invoice for two weeks."

Transformed: "I've been putting off sending that invoice for two weeks."

Exercise 3A — Story prompts

Tell me a story

Choose one prompt. Speak for 60–90 seconds. Your story can be real or invented. You must use at least 3 vocabulary items naturally — not forced at the end, but woven into the story.

1Tell me about a time you had to take on something that felt way too big for you. What happened? Did you stick to the plan or change it along the way?
2Describe a professional relationship where you really struggled to build rapport — with a student, client, or collaborator. What did you try? Did it work?
3Talk about a situation where you had to point out a problem or mistake — to a student, client or colleague. How did you do it? Did the person back down?
4Describe a project or task you had to call off or put aside. What got in the way? How did you feel about it?
5Tell me about a moment in your music or teaching career where you had to cross the divide — connect with someone who saw things completely differently.
🎯

Required: say at least 3 vocabulary words. Suggested: "I had to…", "The situation was…", "What I did was…", "Looking back…"

Exercise 3B — Word chain story

Connect the words

Your teacher gives you 4 vocabulary words in random order. You must tell a short but coherent story that uses all 4, in any order. The story must make logical sense.

Chain A

Setting suggestion: a new job or collaboration

Chain B

Setting suggestion: a very busy season in your business

Chain C

get under your skin · back down · in the same boat · cross the divide

Setting suggestion: a disagreement with a difficult person

Chain D

Setting suggestion: a project that went wrong

Example — Chain A

"When I started a new group online, I was completely frozen out at first. Nobody responded to my messages. I decided to speak up in one of the communities and managed to build rapport with two people. I followed up with both of them and now they're regular students."

Exercise 4A — Hot takes

Agree, disagree, and explain

For each statement: (1) choose an agree/disagree phrase, (2) say it immediately, (3) give at least two reasons using vocabulary from the week. No pausing before you start.

1"Entrepreneurs who can't build rapport with clients will always struggle — no matter how good their product is."
2"If a colleague freezes you out, the best thing to do is confront them directly instead of putting off the conversation."
3"People who always back down in difficult conversations give off a weak impression professionally."
4"You can't really step up in your career if you never speak up in meetings or group situations."
5"Freelancers are all in the same boat — there's no way to completely avoid burning out at some point."
6"The best coping mechanism for a difficult colleague is to simply get on with your work and ignore them."
7"A manager who always points out mistakes but never checks in with the team gives off the wrong energy."

Example response

Statement 1 → "I feel the same, actually. As an English teacher and content creator, I've learned that building rapport is everything. If a student doesn't feel comfortable, they put off practising and you can't really carry out effective lessons. The technical knowledge matters, but the connection matters more."

⚖️

Exercise 4B — Devil's advocate

Defend the opposite side

Your teacher picks a side. You must argue the opposite — even if you personally agree with the original. This trains you to use the vocabulary flexibly and think fast. 40 seconds per round.

Round 1

"You should always speak up when something gets under your skin at work."

Argue: sometimes it's better NOT to speak up

Round 2

"Crossing the divide with a difficult colleague is always worth the effort."

Argue: sometimes it's not worth it at all

Round 3

"Freelancers should never take on more than 3 clients at a time to avoid burnout."

Argue: taking on more is actually fine and necessary

Round 4

"Backing down in a negotiation gives off a weak professional image."

Argue: knowing when to back down is a strength

🎯

You don't have to believe what you're arguing — that's the point. Use: "I see it differently…" or "I'm not so sure about that…" to open.

Exercise 5A — 30-second sprints

Speak first, think second

Your teacher reads a question. You must start speaking within 2 seconds and talk for 30 seconds minimum. The vocabulary word must appear in your first two sentences — not saved for later.

30 seconds · start immediately
1"Tell me about a time you almost burned out. What happened and what coping mechanisms did you use?"
2"Has anyone ever frozen you out professionally? Or have you ever frozen someone out — even unintentionally?"
3"What's one situation where you really had to step up? Did you stick to the original plan or change your approach?"
4"Do you find it easy to speak up when something gets under your skin? Or do you tend to put it off?"
5"Describe someone you really get on with professionally. How did you build rapport with them?"
6"As an infoproducer or teacher, what's one task you often put off even though you know you should carry it out?"
7"Has there been a time you had to cross the divide with a student or client who gave off a difficult energy?"

⚡ Rule: no "hmm", no "well…", no "that's a great question." The first word out of your mouth must be the vocabulary item or an agree/disagree phrase.
